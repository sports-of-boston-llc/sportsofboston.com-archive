Last week, the Red Sox and Dodgers completed a nine-player blockbuster trade that reshaped both teams. The Red Sox unloaded Adrian Gonzalez, Josh Beckett, Nick Punto, and the injured Carl Crawford to the Dodgers in exchange for James Loney and prospects. The biggest return the Red Sox received was that the Dodgers agreed to pay about $260 million of the remaining salary on those contracts. This virtually reset the finances of the Red Sox, giving them the opportunity to restructure the team in the coming offseason. Throughout all the troubles of the 2012 season, there was no question that this Sox team needed to do something drastic to make a change. In every trade there are winners and there are losers, so today we take a look at who fell into which category.

Winners: 2012 Dodgers

The Dodgers have made it clear they want to win and they want to win now. This trade gives them a gold glove defender at first, but also it gives them an offensive threat that they were missing. Adrian Gonzalez originally played for the Padres so he knows the NL West well, and the change of scenery and laid back atmosphere of Los Angeles could be the change he needed to get back to his old ways. (...)

The Boston Red Sox finished off their month of May in suitable fashion. On New Hampshire Day at Fenway Park, the Red Sox beat the Kansas City Royals 8-1 to salvage a split of the series heading into tomorrow’s day off. After a much-needed sweep of the Tampa Bay Rays, the Sox came into this four-game series with the Royals confident and collected. They have been steadily getting healthier (with the exception of the bizarre on-goings of Jacoby Ellsbury and Josh Beckett), and climbing the standings in the very competitive A.L. East.

The first two losses of this series were somewhat overshadowed in this city by the Celtics finally overcoming the Orlando Magic to head back to the finals, but this was a series the Boston could not overlook, as the Royals are right at the top of the league in team batting average. After the most impressive pitching match-up of the weekend lived up to it’s hype on Saturday night, with Clay Buchholz one-upping Zach Greinke, the Sox entered Sunday needing a split to end this impressive month of May on a high note. He’s not alone, though, when it comes to famous athletes with disorders. Ted Johnson suffered from depression after post-concussion syndrome. Zack Greinke suffered from social anxiety disorder. Barret Robbins had Bipolar disorder. And that’s just the beginning of the list.

For the most part, as fans, we look at athletes based on their physical stature and the numbers that they produce while playing. We cheer them on when they do well and sometimes boo them when they don’t perform well. We may taunt them or make up some catchphrase involving their name or nickname. However, what we forget is that there is more to an athlete than just that. After all, an athlete is just doing their job, and they too are just trying to make a living, and their profession just happens to be what they do well. They too must deal with the same issues many of us deal with.
