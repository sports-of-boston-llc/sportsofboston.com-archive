Reasons Why Tom Brady #Deflategate Suspension is Ridiculous

By KC Downey in Featured, NFL, Patriots, Top Story ⋅ May 11, 2015 at 8:54pm ⋅ Post a comment

New England Patriots quarterback Tom Brady was suspended four games by the NFL on Monday, days after the release of the Wells Report, which concluded that Brady was “generally aware” the Patriots probably deflated footballs used in the AFC Championship game against the Colts earlier this year. The Patriots won that game 45-7.

The Patriots were also fined $1 million and docked a first-round pick in next year’s draft and a fourth-round pick in the 2017 draft.

The following is a list of why Brady being suspended for a quarter of the season is asinine, asiten, asileven, asitwelve!

Ray Rice

First, let’s take a step back and examine what we’re talking about here. Brady is accused of being generally aware that Patriots staffers Jim McNally and John Jastremski let a little air out of his footballs after the referees gave them the OK prior to the game.Â  If true — and let’s be honest, it probably is “generally” true, though there is a gray area that I will get into — Brady deserves some sort of punishment. But four games? That’s a quarter of the season, or the equivalent of a 40-game suspension in Major League Baseball or a 20-game suspension in the NBA or NHL.

Four games is also twice as many games as Ravens running back Ray Rice was initially suspended for.

Yes, you read that right. What the NFL believes Brady was generally aware of is twice as worse as what the hotel surveillance tape showed the world – Rice dragging his out-cold fiancee out of an elevator (of course, we later learned he punched her lights our first). The NFL was willing to give Rice the benefit of the doubt for whatever reason — something the league wasn’t willing to do for Brady.

Spygate

In its ruling, the NFL said:

“Here, there are several factors that merit strong consideration in assessing discipline. The first is the club’s prior record. In 2007 the club and several individuals were sanctioned for videotaping signals of opposing defensive coaches in violation of the Constitution and Bylaws. Under the Integrity of the Game Policy, this prior violation of competitive rules was properly considered in determining the discipline in this case.”

I think it’s fair to say that the Patriots’ reputation precedes it, fair or not. And yes, every team in the NFL has a prior transgression and seemingly none of them are punished with that in mind. This really doesn’t seem fair. But, it’s a second significant transgression in the Brady-Belichick-Kraft era, so I feel the fining of the team is totally just. And, taking a draft pick is probably fair, too.

We could argue all day about which pick was more right to take, but that’s not the point here. By every account I’m aware of, Brady had nothing to do with Spygate. That was Bill Belichick planting a camera in a spot where he wasn’t supposed to, per a league memo that year. So, why is Brady being lambasted because his coach (who was not affected at all by this punishment, somehow) did something wrong 8 years ago?

The Gray Area

Anyone with an opinion on Deflategate seems to fall on one side of the argument – whether Brady is guilty or not guilty. This, like almost anything else, is not a black-and-white situation. There’s a huge gray area with the controversy, and there’s a bunch of important context to consider.

My theory is that Brady told Jastremski that he likes his footballs on the lower end of the 12.5-13.5 PSI range. I think this became a big deal during the Jets game in October of this past season, when game officials pumped up Brady’s footballs well past what he likes (per the Wells report). Brady then probably told Jastremski to make sure the balls stay on the lower end.

I also believe that Brady was generally aware that Jastremski and McNally were capable of taking matters into their own hands in the deflating of the footballs. Did he necessarily know they would deflate the footballs after referee inspections each game? That’s hard to say. If he wanted it done by any means necessary, then maybe Jastremski and McNally took him at his word. And if that’s true – that’s on Brady. But we don’t know it’s true, and that’s the point.

The Sting – How serious is this, really?

Until the Colts brought it to the league’s attention, I think the NFL was willing to be lax with inflation rules that have never been enforced in the history of the league. The more comfortable a quarterback is with his footballs, the more likely his team will score, and the more likely he will have more fantasy points. The NFL clearly loves its offense, so why change a thing?

The deflated football rule was hardly enforced during pre-game inspections across the league. This was especially true before the AFC title game, when head official Walt Anderson failed to record what each team football measured at despite prior knowledge that the Patriots might be up to no good.

Perhaps this was a way to catch the Patriots red-handed at halftime — and they sure did. Most of the Patriots’ balls lost a higher percentage of air than the Colts’ balls did — well, we think so anyway, but without a pre-game recording, how do we know where the Patriots team balls started at?

Furthermore, league and game officials allowed the first half of the AFC CHAMPIONSHIP GAME to be played with what they believe were under-inflated footballs. Unreal. As Michael Felger pointed out Monday on 98.5 The Sports Hub, the sting is OK as long as the punishment fits the crime. If the NFL was willing to do a sting, they almost concede that this crime by the Patriots is akin to a thief sneakily stealing the newspaper from the convenience store, and not a robber murdering the store clerk. In other words, don’t catch Brady and the Patriots shoplifting and punish them for murder.

Brady’s Stature

We’ve all heard stories about Packers quarterback Aaron Rodgers liking his footballs to be over-inflated, even telling Green Bay staffers to over-inflate it, if they can. This was shared as a passing conversation by CBS color-commentator Phil Simms in the past couple seasons. So, it’s clear to me that other quarterbacks have their preferences.

Just like I think it would be foolish to think Earth is the only planet in a seemingly infinite universe home to intelligent life, I think it’s stupid to think Brady is the only quarterback in the NFL to be generally aware of rule-bending to get a competitive edge.

Rodgers is one of the best quarterbacks in the NFL, but with all due respect, he’s not in Brady’s league historically. Brady has won four Super Bowls, two league MVPs, three Super Bowl MVPs and is considered by many as the greatest quarterback in league history. It seems clear that the NFL is being especially harsh with Brady because he’s so famous and so damn good.

“Each player, no matter how accomplished and otherwise respected, has an obligation to comply with the rules and must be held accountable for his actions when those rules are violated and the public’s confidence in the game is called into question.”

In other words… “We’ll come down hard on anyone, and especially hard on the best players in our league.”

NFLPA’s Influence

Brady has been criticized nationally for failing to turn over relevant information on his cellphone to NFL investigators, and the NFL cites his perceived noncooperation as more fuel to the harsh punishment:

“Moreover, the report documents your failure to cooperate fully and candidly with the investigation, including by refusing to produce any relevant electronic evidence (emails, texts, etc.), despite being offered extraordinary safeguards by the investigators to protect unrelated personal information, and by providing testimony that the report concludes was not plausible and contradicted by other evidence.”

This is where it becomes a players’ union-league fight. There’s no way the NFLPA would let Brady turn over his cellphone because it would set a precedent for future investigations. The Wells Report failed to mention Brady’s unique situation (being accused of something serious by the NFL while being held back by his union for his protection), and the NFL failed to add this important context. Why? Because the league and the union have a contentious history. It does them no good to concede anything to Brady.

Where the Wind Blows

Like a sailor, the NFL, Commissioner Roger Goodell and NFL Executive President Troy Vincent waited to see which way the wind was blowing before making their ruling. Instead of intently reading the Wells Report cover to cover (as we know, Vincent didn’t read the Mueller report about the Rice investigation), the league likely tuned into ESPN, the NFL Network and national news broadcasts and listened to talk radio shows across the country before making their ruling.

Most outside of New England wanted Brady’s head on a stick. The public got their wish, and Goodell and Vincent can now take their first victory lap in a long time after badly botching the Rice and Adrian Peterson situations.

Future punishments have to be over-the-top like Brady’s suspension because that will appease the general public, even if it’s not in the interest of fairness or the integrity of the sport, which is funny because that’s what league officials were supposedly trying to protect.
