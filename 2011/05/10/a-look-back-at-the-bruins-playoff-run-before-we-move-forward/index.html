A Look Back at the Bruins Playoff Run, Before We Move Forward

By Ryan Hadfield in Bruins, Featured, Now!, Top Story ⋅ May 10, 2011 at 12:00pm ⋅ Post a comment

It’s a great time of year to be a Boston sports fan. I know, I know – I’m not exactly breaking news with that sentiment, but let’s face it, even though it has been nearly 3 years since the city of Boston last celebrated a championship, things are more exciting than ever.

The Patriots were recently dubbed co-favorites by Vegas along with the Green Bay Packers to win the Super Bowl next year (assuming there is one), the Red Sox are slowly (painstakingly slowly actually) starting to piece things together, and lastly – and most importantly – we are treated to a playoff game involving the Bruins or Celtics nearly every night.

Good times.

Let that fester for a minute.

Good? With me? Ok – let’s move on.

Obviously the most significant feature mentioned in the opening paragraph revolves around the  playoff run of our local Ice Hockey team, the Boston Bruins. Friday night at the Garden, in front of a raucous crowd, the Bruins finally circumvented the ghost of second round’s past. You may or may not have heard: something about 3 consecutive years of Game 7 losses leading to a despondent fan base waiting for a collapse to arise this season. Fortunately, this year the Bruins did not satiate that proclivity of failure. They moved on. Got over the hump. Stared at the monkey, smiled, and said, “Sorry no piggyback ride today, George. I’m moving on.”

In short, the Bruins graduated.

After reading a nifty 8,000 word column from ESPN’s Bill Simmons about the NBA Playoffs and The Wire, I thought it would be fun to do something similar with our beloved Boston Bruins. And that last statement about graduation gave me the idea I was looking for…

So without further adieu, let’s parallel one of the greatest high school summer movies of all time with the B’s, in a class superlatives fashion…Here are your Dazed & Confused/Boston Bruins awards..

Don Dawson (Sasha Jenson)– Don was the lovable sidekick of star quarterback Pink. He does everything from hitting on his teacher to antagonizing upcoming freshman. He says things like, “When I get to college, all I’m gonna do is bang, bang, bannnnnngg!” Overall good vibes are omnipresent whenever Don’s on screen.

Bruins Winner: I feel like whenever Dennis Seidenberg is on the ice for Boston good things tend to happen. Additionally, he’s Big Z’s trusty blue line side-kick.  He pinches deep into the opposition’s zone at the right times, and forces the action….much like Don presumes to force the action in college.

Randall “Pink” Floyd (Jason London) – Pink is the indifferent star quarterback in Dazed. He spends half the movie dodging a pledge mandated by the coaching staff to not drink or do drugs prior to or during the season; and the other half having a clairvoyant aura about him while being like-able. Not only is Pink is calm, cool, and collective at the most stressful times (like when an old guy is threatening to call the cops on him and his friends for destroying a mailbox), but he also bites his tongue for much of the movie which makes his actual lines more poignant. He takes a hazed freshman (Mitch) under his wing for the night to show him the brighter side of high school in the small town, goes with the flow, and has an altruistic attitude but only to a point before he starts to deviate.

Bruins Winner: Calm, cool, and collective? Tim Thomas was all three of those things and then some in both the first and second round series victories. His play is unpredictable in a good way – half the time you don’t understand how he makes the plays he does. He’s truly been transcendent. This unpredictability matches Pink’s unwillingness to sign the form issued by his coaches. Thomas carried the Bruins to victory in Game 2 against the Flyers. The team should have lost, and come back to Boston with the series tied at one game apiece. Thomas had other plans.

Mitch Kramer (Wiley Wiggins) – Mitch is the second lead in the film. He’s the goofy 8th grader entering his first year of high school. He struggles avoiding the inevitable hazing from his future peers in the beginning of the film. Though, ultimately, Mitch acts at an age beyond his years throughout the film under the tutelage of Pink, Don, and even Wooderson.  By the movies end Mitch manages to hook up with a girl two years older than him (an impressive coup), gets caught coming home at the crack of dawn by his mother without any consequences (ditto), and earns the respect of everyone after playing a prank on the maligned 5th year senior, Obannion.

Bruins Winner: Our first dual award goes to Brad Marchand and Nathan Horton.  Both are 22 and 25 respectively and seeing their first extensive playoff action, all-the-while delivering when it counts the most. Marchand has been a pest to opposing team’s. His nose has been the subject of a dubious tweet, much like Mitch’s playing with his hair every 3.5 seconds is a point of contention in Dazed. Meanwhile, Horton has been the paradigm of clutch. He plays his best in paramount situations, scoring two overtime goals in the Montreal series, and stalling before delivering an exemplar lead pass to Milan Lucic to put Game 4 away last night.

David Wooderson (Matthew McConaughey) – A breakout role for the athiest of t-shirts and ladies man. This is probably the first and last time guys can find McConaughey enjoyable. In the movie, he’s the resident 22 year-old who is still hanging out with 17 year-olds, which is okay – after all – he likes being with high school girls because, “I get older. And they stay the same age.”

Bruins Winner: Easy. The ageless one, Mark Recchi. Recchi is 42, and still an effective force on the B’s second line. Like the iconic Wooderson, Recchi is an old guy in a young man’s game. He’s a sage in a every sense of the word, and by all accounts a locker room glue-guy.

Ron Slater (Rory Cochrane)– Slater was the uber burn-bag in Dazed (which is sort of a double-entendre, since potheads typically are the only people that actually use the phrase ‘uber.’ Note – somehow they’ve adopted epic from the rest of society as well.). Slater is always enjoyable and he says things like:

“Check yaaa later”

“Behind every good man there is a woman, and that woman was Martha Washington, man, and everyday George would come home, she would have a big fat bowl waiting for him, man, when he come in the door, man, she was a hip, hip, hip lady, man.”

“Didja ever look at a dollar bill, man? There’s some spooky shit goin’ on there. And it’s green too.”

And has the following exchange with Mitch:

Slater: This place used to be off limits, man, ’cause some drunk freshman fell off. He went right down the middle, smacking his head on every beam, man. I hear it doesn’t hurt after the first couple though. Autopsy said he had one beer, how many did you have?

Mitch: Four.

Slater: You’re dead, man, you’re so dead. Look at the blood stains right there

Probably the most memorable performance in the film, and often is overlooked.

Bruins Winner: To the two players who are pure anchors to the team – Zdeno Chara and Patrice Bergeron. Admittedly, both are definitely stars so I don’t think they are overlooked, but I feel like we take them for granted – especially Big Z. We abhor his lack of leadership, which is particularly damming since, you know, he’s the captain and everything. However, we forget, this is a guy who is renowned for his work ethic. How many times have we heard how Chara is the first guy on the ice and the last off, at virtually every Bruins practice? Additionally, Bergeron was the only Bruin to show up the first two games of the Montreal series. He’s playing the type of two-way hockey that Charlie Conway eventually embraces at the end of Might Ducks 3.

Kevin Pickford (Shawn Andrews)– Pickford is the resident drug dealer, who is planning a big end of school year blow-out at his parents house while they are going away. His plans are foiled when the three kegs he ordered arrive early, and his dad calls off the trip. My favorite part of the exchange is how Pickford’s mother is so nonchalant, and not at all demur about wanting to still leave the house alone to her son. Really? Three kegs? The driver ”confusing the address”? None of these are red flags? Well, thankfully, Wooderson plans a massive rager in the woods in lieu of Pickfords failures..

Bruins Winner: Last year, as the Bruins were committing a historical collapse at the hands of the Flyers, everyone and their dog pointed to the David Krejci injury as the turning point of the series. A year later – like Pickford – Krejci would get his party after recovering from his wrist injury. He was seen jawing back and forth with Philly players throughout the four-game sweep, who were apparently giving him crap about bowing out in last year’s series. Well, Krejci had the last laugh this time around. He was a catalyst throughout the duration of the series. He scored the overtime winner in Game 2; and had a big assist to open the scoring in Game 4’s contest.

Fred O’Bannion (Ben Affleck)– A lot of people forget about this role for Affleck. But he shines doing what he does best – being an a-hole. He spends his time terrorizing incoming freshmen, inexplicably driving like a maniac, and being detestedby everyone else. Everyone has a friend like O’Bannion. For some reason you keep him around, even though he has no redeeming qualities about him. He’s best described by Pink when he says, “Yeah, he’s kind of a joke. But he’s not a bad guy to have blocking for you.”

Bruins Winner – This is award is detrimental, and definitely not a positive. I feel like the power play will eventually improve, but the B’s fourth line is putrid. In Game 2, while the team was reeling and just trying to get out of the 3rd period tied, they turned to the little used 4th line to give the team minutes. The move nearly cost the team the game. I understand, the crash line is a supplementary tool in the playoffs that shouldn’t be a unit of exigence, but I feel like going forward the Bruins need to depend on this unit in dire times when they have no other option. And if that performance is indictative of what they are going to provide, something needs to change.

Though, I suppose, it’s an advantageous sign that my one gripe with the team is it’s fourth line. With that said, bring on Tampa.
