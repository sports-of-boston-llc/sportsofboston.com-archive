9 Things to Look For at Red Sox Spring Training

February 23, 2016 at 9:26pm in Featured, Opinion, Red Sox, Top Story
Brendan Gallagher writes
Add a comment

Now that we have officially made it past the official report date for all Red Sox players, fans can finally start to look forward to Opening Day 2016. Spring Training is usually more of a fleeting tease than legitimate action, but there if one pays attention there are still lessons to be learned. With that in mind, we’ve come up with a list of nine items/storylines to keep track of down in Ft. Myers (we’ve intentionally excluded Pablo Sandoval‘s weight problem, because that should surprise no one).

  1. Other options at Third Base – Sandoval’s conditioning and physical shape are always going to be a problem, so no one should have been shocked when he showed up looking like he had just eaten Dustin Pedroia for breakfast and Brock Holt for lunch. Sandoval’s contract also essentially makes the starting job at Third his to lose, but there is a possibility that he takes himself out of the running for everyday playing time if his performance falls off a cliff. It would be interesting to know who might stand to benefit from more available playing time at the hot corner should the need arise. The early guess would most likely be Holt, due to his experience at the position, although Travis Shaw saw limited action at Third last season so he at least has experience there as well.
  2. How will the bridge to Craig Kimbrel be composed – We already know that the Red Sox main setup core will consist of at least Carson Smith and Koji Uehara, with Junichi Tazawa also likely to see some setup work as well. What will be interesting to see is how exactly those pitchers are deployed Early reports have Uehara scheduled to pitch the eighth inning, while Smith and Tazawa split duties in earlier innings as the situation demands. However, an argument could be made that it might be better for the Red Sox to more evenly divide the setup duties between those three relievers based more on situations and health, in order to keep everyone fresh deep into the season. Of course, this will also depend on how deep into games Red Sox starters can manage to go. The bottom line is that Uehara is coming off of a pretty major injury going into his age-41 season, Tazawa was shut down last season due to fatigue, and Smith is too good to relegate to middle-relief innings.
  3. Who plays most in the Outfield – We already know that some combination of Rusney Castillo, Jackie Bradley Jr., Mookie Betts, and Chris Young will be taking almost all of the time in the Outfield, but it is still fairly wide open as to how starts will be distributed among those players. Young will most likely see just about any left-handed starter, so that takes one of the other three out of the lineup. Betts will probably get the most starts, since he is a dynamic leadoff piece and fits perfectly in front of Pedroia and Xander Bogaerts. It seems like Castillo is the x-factor here, since he is still relatively unknown and has just as much bust potential as boom potential.
  4. Who wins the fifth starter job – Unfortunately this is probably Joe Kelly‘s job to lose. He has a “live arm” and “great stuff,” and all of those other cliche’s baseball people like to use, but Kelly has been mediocre at best since coming to Boston. It is true that Kelly can throw hard and look dominant at times, but it is also true that he has absolutely no ability to read hitters or keep them off balance. Kelly is more of a thrower than a pitcher, and there is no reason why he should be guaranteed a rotation job this season. The Red Sox owe it to themselves to at least let one of the the younger pitchers like Henry Owens or Brian Johnson compete for a rotation spot in camp.
  5. How long of a leash does Joe Kelly get – In the likely event that Kelly does win a rotation spot, how long can he struggle before the Red Sox decide enough is enough? We have already covered how the Red Sox bullpen is improved, but it will not be able to stand Kelly lasting three to four innings per start every fifth day like he has in the past. The Red Sox do have alternatives available if Kelly struggles, and if they are serious about winning in 2016, they will not hesitate to go to them if necessary.
  6. How will the catching situation play out – This is a topic we will go into a bit more depth on later in the week, but it bears watching. The Red Sox have two of the best young catchers in the game in Blake Swihart and Christian Vazquez, but Swihart is still young and Vazquez is coming off of a major injury. This makes it likely that Vazquez will start the season in Pawtucket, with Swihart and Ryan Hanigan sharing time behind the dish. But if Vazquez plays well in Pawtucket, or if the other two struggle while Vazquez proves he is healthy, there could be an uncomfortable catching situation at Fenway Park this summer.
  7. Will the Red Sox add another left-handed reliever – There is no real need to do this yet, since guys like Roenis Elias, Tommy Layne, and Robbie Ross will all be in camp. Those are not household names by any stretch, but the Red Sox already have their most important relief innings taken care of, so they should need to worry less about a dominating lefty as long as the guys they do have can do a respectable job in the earlier innings.
  8. Does Hanley Ramirez even know what a First Baseman’s mitt looks like – Hanley could make or break the Red Sox’ season at First Base, but for the love of all that is good can he at least get his own glove? Hanley has two only two simple responsibilities for the Red Sox in 2016: rake at the plate, and catch the ball when people throw it at him. Even he should be able to figure those out.
  9. How painful will David Ortiz‘s “farewell tour” be – Extremely. It was bad enough having to hear about the Derek Jeter nonsense and it seems like these farewell tours have become some kind of PR monster. Can’t wait until the Oakland A’s give Ortiz a chair to sit in while his teammates play defense, or the Tampa Bay Rays give him some kind of orange-based thing to stay healthy while he bashes their pitchers all over the ballpark.

Fantasy Baseball Outlook: First Base

February 23, 2016 at 5:10pm in Fantasy, MLB
bmckenney writes
Add a comment

This analysis offers a tiered ranking system of first basemen for the upcoming fantasy baseball season. It assumes a 16 team mixed-league keeper format with a standard 5×5 scoring system and a 22 man roster with the following positional requirements: C (1), 1B (1), 2B (1), 3B (1), SS (1), CI (1), MI (1), OF (3), UT (3), SP (5), RP (4). All statistical information is referenced from FanGraphs.com.

 

Paul Goldschmidt (ARI)

Not much needs to be said here. Goldy is arguably the most valuable fantasy player and is only 28 years old. It will be fun to watch him lead a Diamondbacks team with World Series aspirations.

Anthony Rizzo (CHC), Jose Abreu (CWS)

The 26 year old Florida native is another stud. He is the cornerstone of what may become the Cubs team to break the Curse, and will hit in the middle of their lineup for the foreseeable future. Don’t expect the 17 stolen bases he had in 2015, but he is a high caliber talent worthy of your first round draft selection. 

Jose Abreu gets included in this tier because at 29 he is reaching his prime as a first baseman, and in his only two seasons has hit 30 home runs with 100 RBI. This production may not rise any more, but is an absolute asset to fantasy owners as he reaches his prime as a first-basemen.

Miguel Cabrera (DET), Edwin Encarnacion (TOR)

It’s hard to argue that Miggy is not the best hitter in baseball, but what separated him from the likes of Rizzo and Goldschmidt is the injury risk. Cabrera is 33 years old and has had trouble staying healthy in recent years. A luxury when he stays on the field but his ability to do so has become a concern.

Edwin Encarnacion is a similar case. Offers four category production when healthy but struggled with some wrist and hand injuries and is not getting any younger. 

Joey Votto (CIN), Eric Hosmer (KC)

Votto is a model of consistency. He is a pure line drive hitter that reaps the benefits of Great American Ballpark and it seems like he is always on-base thanks to his 21% walk rate. His value is degraded slightly by the lousy Cincinnati lineup this season but he is still a fantasy asset.

Hosmer is a player on the rise after his performance last season and is hitting the stride of his career. His steady contact and line drive rates have helped him maintain a good average and there remains some untapped power potential.

Miguel Sano (MIN), Freddie Freeman (ATL), David Ortiz (BOS)

Sano is oozing upside despite the fact that he has yet to manage MLB pitching enough to maintain a decent average, He has keeper value rooted in the elite raw power and young age, but he needs to find a position to play on the diamond so that he does not have assume a strictly DH role.

Freddie Freeman will have the unfortunate duty of hitting in the middle of the Braves lineup this year. Pitchers are likely to pitch around him and there no one behind him likely to produce on a consistent basis. He is  really good line drive hitter entering his prime but the problem is his supporting cast. A keeper, but he will not be able to do it all himself this season. 

Last here is Big Papi. I acknowledge this is a generous ranking in his last year before retirement, but a lock to hit 30 HR with 100 RBI in his send-off tour, he deserves nothing less.

Adrian Gonzalez (LAD), Prince Fielder (TEX), Kendrys Morales (KC)

Adrian Gonzalez is a pure line-drive hitter that stabilizes the middle of the Dodgers lineup. Unfortunately he is on the wrong side of 30 and although a proven fantasy asset he does not offer the same potential as other options. 

Prince Fielder is in a similar situation. He bounced back nicely at the start of last season from neck surgery but failed to maintain that performance in the second half of the season. He can hit when he stays healthy but, like Gonzalez, his best years are behind him. 

Morales is aging too. Now 33 he has seen his peak years in the rear-view mirror. He remains a is a solid utility option that can boost the HR, RBI categories while maintaining a respectable batting average.

Albert Pujols (LAA), Brandon Belt (SF), Carlos Santana (CLE)

Pujols will provide power and power. His line drive rate was its lowest in five years last season (16%) as he hit for an abysmal .244 batting average. He did hit 40 home runs last year, but at 36 years old he is no longer the machine he once was. 

Belt is the youngest of the group that is a contributor when on the field. But concussion problems are well-noted and make him a wild card at this point. This is confusing option for fantasy owners. A line drive hitter with some contact issues at timees, he offers surplus value if he falls in the draft because of health concerns. 

The last on the list is Carlos Santana. He fits a power profile with a lack of batting average but supports his value with very good OBP and the opportunity he will have to drive in runs from his spot in the Indians’ lineup. 

Mark Teixeira (NYY), Evan Gattis (HOU)

I am biased against anyone on the Yankees roster but Teixeira deserves a spot on here because he’s a proven veteran that benefits from playing in the new Yankee Stadium. If he can stay on the field he offers owners a nice value pick opportunity.

Evan Gattis’ fantasy value has dropped because of his status as a pure designated hitter with the Astros but his offensive output seemed to benefit from the change. One concern is a sports hernia that will delay his 2016 debut until after opening day. He will hurt a team’s OBP but make significant contributions in the home-run and RBI categories.

Ryan Zimmerman (WAS), Lucas Duda (NYM)

If Zimmerman can find a way to stay healthy he will serve as a run producer in the heart of the National’s lineup this season. The problem is his health; he hasn’t logged over 400 at-bats in either of the last two season so it’s hard to anticipate a valuable season from him.

Teixeria’s New York City counterpart, Luca Duda, is a pure power hitter. He won’t hit for an average but has shown promising improvement in his OBP over the last few years and is a mid-level fantasy option 

This class of first basemen offer owners a wide range of quality options depending on the roster you have put together. At the top are bonafide fantasy baseball studs that can anchor a team through the season, and are absolute keepers worthy of top draft picks. If you miss out on those guys and get your fantasy star at another position there are other first basemen that make solid contributions. As expected, most of these guys will produce for power categories like home runs, and RBI and can provide decent production in a utility role for almost any team.

Fishing for Prospects: A.J. Reed (HOU)

If you only take only one thing away from this entire series, let it be this name because A.J. Reed is poised for stardom. He was selected 42nd overall selection in the 2014 MLB draft after a stunning season at Kentucky that culminated in the Golden Spikes Award. That season he led the nations in home runs with 23 with a .336 batting average, and also contributed a 12-2 record on the mound with a 2.09 ERA and 71 strikeouts. The Astros player development staff decided to commit to Reed as a hitter and he thrived in the Class A Advanced California league before earning a promotion to Double-A in the second half of the season. Headed in to 2015 the Astros have other options ahead of him, but depending on how spring training unfolds Reed may get a shot to prove himself with the major league club as soon as this season.

Meet the Prospects: Michael Chavis

February 18, 2016 at 6:37pm in Red Sox
bmckenney writes
Add a comment

Michael Chavis, the 26th overall selection in the 2014 MLB Draft, was drafted out of Sprayberry High School in Marietta, Georgia. A highly touted infielder for his natural athleticism and raw power, Chavis has yet to live up to the high expectations.

He has a balanced stance from the right handed batters box with his hands steady at shoulder level, and starts his swing with a short, quite stride. He unleashes his hands through the zone with impressive bat speed and scouts regard his raw power potential.

Growing up Chavis played primarily shortstop but when he was draft it was unclear whether he would stick at the position long-term. Last season Red Sox management decided to move him to third-base on a permanent based on their projection of Chavis’ player profile. Unfortunately this transition has not gone smoothly as hoped.

In 39 games at the end of 2014 in rookie league with the GCL Red Sox, he had a decent performance but was not overwhelming. He spent time at both shortstop and third-base in that campaign and posted a respectable .767 OPS with one home-run and 16 RBI.  His trouble was in the field as he made seven errors in 39 games. Then last year, with the class-A Greenville Drive, he seemed to regress further. In 68 games at the hot corner he made 19 errors resulting in a dismal .907 fielding percentage. Chavis needs to work on his technique at the new position and it must be addressed if he wants to progress as a professional, but it is a skill that he can explicitly focus on and improve if he puts in the hard work.

As mentioned, the 20 year old prospect is heralded for his ability to hit the long-ball. Last season with the Drive he hit 16 home-runs and posted an impressive .182 ISO. The concern however is his approach in the batter’s box; although he hit those home runs, he appended the power output with a lousy 67% contact rate and 6% walk rate. This led to a disquieting .223/.277/.405 slash line.

In his limited professional exposure there are no doubts about Chavis’ power, but the lack of plate discipline and feel for the strike-zone leave more to be desired. This is an aspect of baseball that does not come naturally to many but often improves as players accrue more plate appearances. It clicks for some prospects and not for others so by no means a presumed outcome for Chavis. He really needs to address this facet of his offensive toolbox if he wants to keep up his prospect stock.

Chavis is remains a young prospect very early in his development and despite last year’s struggles has plenty of time to improve upon the flaws in his game. Raw power is a valued tool in the baseball industry and there is no doubt he has it. 2016 is an important year for him as a professional and he needs to establish himself as a contributor in the Greenville lineup.

He remains years away from a debut at Fenway Park and hasn’t earned spotlight recognition from Red Sox Nation, but prospect junkies will be sure to have an eye on him in the next few of years. If he addresses the holes in his game and develops as a player I will expect to see him at Hadlock Field by late 2017 or early 2018.

Meet the Prospects: Anderson Espinoza

January 30, 2016 at 5:04pm in Boston, Red Sox
bmckenney writes
Add a comment

I have to be honest, I have a really hard time following and writing about the NBA. Even when it is the Celtics. I’ve written a couple posts about them, but I lack the same passion that I have for baseball. So, in response to this epiphany, I have decided to start a new segment for spring training focused on Red Sox prospects. These are some guys that Red Sox Nation should keep their eyes on in this spring and in the coming years. Because of all the hype Yoan Moncada and Rafael Devers have gotten since his signing I will skip him and save you the redundancy by simply saying: they are really promising prospects. To lead off the series this week’s post will feature Venezuelan RHP Anderson Espinoza, who was recently rated the #39 on the 2016 MLB Top Prospect List.

The Red Sox signed the 16-year old Espinoza for $1.8 million in the summer of 2014. At the time he threw his fastball 90-92, with a decent slider and makings of a change-up. In this past season at 17 years of age, his first as a professional in the United States, he was sitting 94-96 on the gun and touching 98. Pretty good for the equivalent of a junior in high school.

Espinoza has a wiry build at 6 feet tall and 170 pounds and looks like natural athlete on the mound. From the windup he as a simple low leg rock with an ever so slight pause at the top before he break his hands. This simple, efficient motion helps him repeat his delivery well for someone his age. Up to this point in his life he has been able to rely heavily on his explosive fastball without much need for a secondary pitch, and had a thorough debut in Rookie and low Class-A ball last year to the tone of a 2.16 ERA in the 15 starts. The next step in his progression will be to put on some muscle, develop a feel for his change-up and, like every young pitcher, improve his command of all three pitches.

Red Sox fans should not expect Espinoza to debut at Fenway Park anytime soon. He needs to put on some mass and develop as pitcher not just a thrower. It is likely he needs time in Class-A ball for a year or so before coming to Portland, but even that may still keep him on pace to debut at Fenway before his first legal beer. Expect him to start the year in Greenville with a possible of promotion to Salem by the end of the season. Plans for Espinoza are long term because he is so young, but with a special talent like his the sky is the limit.
