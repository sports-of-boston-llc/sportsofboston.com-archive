Joe West is Right About Sox, Yankees: These Games are Slow!

April 9, 2010 at 5:26am in Featured, Opinion, Red Sox
Matt Goisman writes
Add a comment

ESPN.com reported Thursday that umpire Joe West believes Red Sox and Yankees games take too long to play. He called the games “embarrassing” and “a disgrace to baseball.” These comments came in response to Tuesday night’s game, in which umpire Angel Hernandez refused on several occasions to grant extra time to batters during their at-bats. Player responses to these comments were almost unilaterally negative, citing that if someone has something better to do, let he or she go and do that. The consensus is that no one is forcing people to stay through the entire game, so what right do the umpires have to criticize the pace?

The Downside of Slow Games

The fact is, Red Sox-Yankees games are too slow. There are too many visits to the mound. Too many step-outs during at-bats. Too many pitching changes. Every Boston-New York game is played as if it’s the end of the world. Both teams are stocked with good, patient hitters, so walks and full-count at-bats are inevitable. But more SHOULD be done by both teams and the umpires to speed up the game when it’s possible. Add a comment

A lot is being made over David Ortiz’s “struggles” over the first two games. At 0-7 with one walk, Ortiz did not shine (that’s for sure). In the third game of the series on Wednesday night, however, he delivered the only RBI of the game when he lined a single over Robinson Cano’s head. While he also hit the ball hard in his first at bat, Ortiz produced nothing the rest of the game, and is just 1/11 so far (.091 AVG).

Ortiz is under a microscope right now, and other than a home run against the Twins at the end of the spring, he isn’t offering too much promise leading into the regular season. It’s too early to tell though. As you may remember, he got hot in the second half last season, even though he killed the Sox production in April and May.

People have a right to be talking about Papi’s issues. Every time he gets up to the plate, the elephant in the room is the fact that he’s hurting the Sox if he’s not putting up the numbers he’s paid to put up. Granderson Spoils Lackey Debut With HR in 10th

April 8, 2010 at 6:05am in Featured, Red Sox, Top Story
KC Downey writes
Add a comment

Despite the difference in payrolls and overall talent, there’s usually not that much separating the Red Sox and Yankees. 2010 is no different, as both teams entered Wednesday night’s game at 1-1. Game 3 of the series and season, of course, was knotted at 1-1 in extra innings when new Yankee Curtis Granderson stepped up to the plate to face Sox closer Jonathan Papelbon.

“If someone scuffles a little bit, people start asking a lot of questions,” Derek Jeter said about getting off to a fast start as a Yankee, referring to Granderson. “Us, as players, we realize you’re going to have ups and downs throughout the course of the year. So we really don’t pay much attention to it, but I’m sure if you’re answering questions about it all the time, it probably gets annoying.”

Granderson avoided those “annoying” post-game questions from reporters by blasting a go-ahead solo home run off Papelbon in the 10th inning. A second run scored after a groundout, three walks, and an RBI groundout from Mark Teixeira, and the Yankees left Boston with a 3-1 win and a 2-1 series edge. Scutaro Error Proves Costly in Red Sox Loss

April 7, 2010 at 6:40am in Featured, Red Sox, Top Story
Matt Goisman writes
Add a comment

Tuesday night saw the continuation of the opening series between the Boston Red Sox and the New York Yankees. The pitching match-up featured both teams’ No. 2 starters, as Jon Lester for the Red Sox and A.J. Burnett for the Yankees pitched in a back-and-forth battle. The Red Sox jumped on the scoreboard in the first inning, however the Yankees tied it up quickly thereafter. Boston put up two more runs in the third, but New York took the lead with three runs of their own two innings later. Boston tied it up in the bottom of the fifth, but the Yankees put up single runs in the seventh and eighth innings to take the lead for good.

The key play of the game was Marco Scutaro’s erroneous throw in the seventh inning. On a routine ball hit to him, he fielded it cleanly, however he then threw it into the dirt trying to get it to first. This allowed the Yankees to load the bases (instead of ending the inning), and they scored on a Hideki Okajima (who took the loss for this game) bases-loaded walk. Beckett and Red Sox Agree to Four-Year Deal

April 5, 2010 at 6:48pm in Featured, Red Sox, Top Story
Pete Rampino writes
Add a comment

In a move we expected to happen, Josh Beckett will be staying in Boston for years to come. Beckett and the Sox came to terms this afternoon on a four-year, $68 million deal that will keep him here through the 2014 season.

The move comes less than 24 hours after Beckett turned in a poor performance against the Yankees in the Sox season opener on Sunday night. Beckett allowed five earned runs in 4.2 innings, but ended up with a no-decision after a late Sox rally.

“We couldn’t be more thrilled to keep Josh around. We were talking that he’ll be with the Red Sox for nine years when this contract is up. He’s earned it. Josh is an excellent contributor on and off the field, a big part of our team and pitching staff.” – Red Sox GM Theo Epstein

The deal cements the Sox rotation as one of the most talented in baseball as Beckett, Jon Lester, and John Lackey will anchor the staff for the next several seasons.
