Patriots’ Defense to Face one of Season’s Toughest Tests in Bears

By Jon Lyons (@TheRealJonLyons) in Featured, Opinion, Patriots, Top Story ⋅ October 22, 2014 at 11:45am ⋅ Post a comment

The Bears will travel to Gillette Stadium on Sunday to face the Patriots and will bring with them one of the most difficult to defend offenses in football. Their mix of dominant wide receivers, and elite running back, and matchup nightmare tight end make them a challenge for any defense. The wild card is their quarterback who can both destroy his opponents or his own offense. This should be a fun match up to watch across the board and the Patriots defense will have to come with their A game to slow the Bears down.

Every offense starts at the quarterback position and the Bears have the enigmatic Jay Cutler at the helm. When playing well, Cutler can carve up defenses and look like one the best quarterbacks in the National Football League but he has a habit of having off days or throwing inexplicable interceptions that kill drives and cost games. He is blessed with some of the best offensive weapons of any quarterback yet he does not consistently take advantage of them. He becomes rattled when pressured and can at times look disinterested, so the Patriots’ pass rush will be key in Sunday’s tilt. If the Patriots can get to Cutler consistently, especially from the up the middle, they will disrupt his timing and throw off the entire Bears offense. Cutler had a poor game against Miami and was reportedly called out by wide receiver Brandon Marshall after the game. Given that Cutler usually doesn’t have two poor games in a row and that Marshall called him out, the Patriots will likely see his best on Sunday.

The Bears receiving corps present one of the best duos in the NFL: Alshon Jeffrey and Brandon Marshall. They both can challenge a defense at all three levels of the passing game (short, intermediate, deep) as well as win jump balls and be tough matchups in the red zone. Essentially, they are both big, strong, fast, do-it-all receivers who can change games by themselves. Marshall checks in at 6’4” and Jeffrey at 6’3”, so having some of the Patriots’ smaller corners such as Alfonzo Dennard or Logan Ryan will be a matchup that favors Chicago. This will therefore be a big game for both Darrelle Revis and Brandon Browner. Browner saw his first action in a limited role in a win against the Jets last Thursday and will likely be called upon for a heavy load. Revis and Browner are the only Patriot corners with the size, speed, and physicality to match up with Jeffrey and Marshall. Revis should be able to consistently disrupt his man but Browner could go either way. He has the tools to play well but looked like he wasn’t fully in football shape last Thursday and it is likely the Bears will test him early. If he can pass that test it will ease the burden on the rest of the defense. Devin McCourty will also have to turn in a strong performance playing behind Revis and Browner. Both of these receivers warrant safety help over the top and McCourty will have to choose wisely who to help on each play. If he guesses wrong and a corner gets beat, the Bears will be putting points on the board. Jeffrey and Marshall are both unselfish on the field which means the Bears can choose to feature both of them or use one a s a decoy. This flexibility keeps defenses guessing and reacting instead of attacking.

The Bears are not the only team with a great wide receiver duo. They are however, the only team to compliment that duo with an elite running back. Matt Forte burst onto the NFL scene in 2008 and instantly established himself as a dominant dual threat. He racked up 1,238 rushing yards and 477 receiving yards in his rookie campaign. He has only gone up from there, registering at least 1,000 all-purpose yards in each of his first six seasons. This year he is at it again with 436 receiving and 438 rushing yards through seven games. The reason Forte is so dominant in both the passing and running game is his physical tools and intelligence. He is durable and has the speed to take a screen pass for 60 yards and strong enough to convert on a third and one run up the middle. His vision is his best asset as he can see holes before the open and has mastered cut back lanes. This allows the Bears to use Forte on all three downs and a defense never knows what is coming when he is on the field. The Patriots run defense will need to play their best game of the season just to slow him down. Vince Wilfork and Casey Walker will play in the middle with Chandler Jones and Rob Ninkovich on the ends. The play of these four will make a difference in both the passing and running game when it comes to Forte. If they get pushed back or leave large open lanes on run plays Forte will be off to the races. If they get too far upfield on screen plays, Forte will be at the second level of the defense before they have a chance to turn around.

Martellus Bennet is often overlooked in the Bears’ offense but he can be a difference maker. His 6’6”, 265 pound frame can shield nearly every defender that covers him from the ball making him a player that can wreak havoc in the middle of the field and in the red zone. The Patriots could try to cover him with linebacker Jamie Collins or potentially use a strong safety such as Patrick Chung or Tavon Wilson. A corner could be brought in as well, such as Kyle Arrington. Whichever route the Patriots choose to go, they cannot overlook Bennett as he will be the story of the game if they do.

The Bears are a team poised to rebound from a poor performance against Miami last week. That starts with their offense and their ability to put points on the board. The Patriots are beginning their toughest stretch of the season as they will play the Bears, Broncos, Colts, Lions, Packers, and Chargers in the next seven weeks with their bye week coming after Denver. It is important that they begin this stretch on a winning note and slowing down the Bears offense will be the key to doing that.
