What Good Play from Betts, Cespedes Means for Crowded Outfield

By mhilperts in Featured, Opinion, Red Sox, Top Story ⋅ September 5, 2014 at 12:30pm ⋅ Post a comment

After Jacoby Ellsbury left the Red Sox to join the Yankees, Boston fans, including myself, shirked off the Center Fielder’s departure as ultimately inconsequential because of our inflated optimism in Jackie Bradley Jr.  We convinced ourselves that Bradley Jr. was our farm system’s answer to fill Ellsbury’s void and that sharing a position with him was going to impede on his development as a major league player. At that point, our mindset was also of the belief that since the majority of our core from last year’s title was still there, only minor changes to the lineup were in order.  To put it lightly, things fell apart.

Production across the lineup decreased while the list of names on the disabled list increased.  Bogaerts, after a promising beginning of the season, lost a touch on the offensive side.  Bradley Jr., on the other hand, appears to have never had a touch on the offensive side from the beginning, and now with news of his refusing to change his swing, the minor leagues appears to be his destination for the time being.

While Bradley Jr. is undeniably a defensive stud, his lack of development on the offensive side was one of the many unforeseen factors in our historically abysmal attempt at defending a championship.  Our idealized starting outfield before the season was with Nava in left, Bradley Jr. in center and Victorino in right.  As of September 3rd, Nava has switched to right, former Athletics-slugger Cespedes is in left, and rookie Mookie Betts in center.  Things have not exactly worked out the way we had imagined them, yet, there are many silver linings that have come out of this season that will certainly contribute to making the Red Sox are more formidable opponent in future seasons.

These silver linings, personified in the forms of Yoenis Cepedes, Mookie Betts, Rusney Castillo, Brock Holt and Joe Kelly, abilities were highlighted (with the exception of Castillo who’s doing just fine in the farm system) last night in the Red Sox’s win over the New York Yankees last night.  Cespedes batted 3 for 5 last night, scoring two runs as well as batting one in, while fellow outfielder Mookie Betts totaled a career-high 3 hits in addition to hitting his fourth home run of his career.  While Holt laid an egg at the plate, his talents were displayed in the infield as he turned two double plays, one of which was significantly difficult.  Joe Kelly looked poised and confident last night, which is a good sign, considering we may be depending on him, especially if we cannot re-acquire Lester next year.  Also noteworthy is Bogaerts’ explosive night, in which he went 4-5, also totaling a career-high in hits during a single game.

So if this production out of recent acquisitions plus young core is a sign of things to come, then what is to happen to our current-tenured stars?  I am specifically referring to the situation we have in the outfield.  We simply do not have enough roster spots for Victorino, Bradley Jr., Nava, Betts, Cespedes, Castillo, and Craig.  That means Cherington has options since I’m of the opinion that several of those guys have legitimate trade value, especially Cespedes and Bradley Jr.

Scenario One:  Trade Victorino and Nava.  This would also mean that Bradley Jr.’s situation would be put on the back burner since we would continue to let him develop in the minor leagues.  However, Betts would essentially behave as his replacement if he can maintain a batting average around .280.  His fielding is inferior to Bradley Jr.’s but it is still solid enough since the disparity in their hitting ability is too large to ignore.  This scenario would also mean that Holt would become a full-time infielder, thus, this easiest, least-dramatic change would be to trade Middlebrooks.  Thus, the outfield would consist of Betts, Castillo and Cespedes while the infield would feature Napoli, Pedroia, Bogaerts and Holt.

Scenario 2:  Trade Victorino and Pedroia.  This one hurt, because it’s Pedroia we’re talking about, but since he signed his contract, his production has plummeted.  It would certainly benefit our future since his contract will likely become an albatross if his production keeps on sinking, so it makes sense on the level void of feelings.  Nava would also have to quit stinking and Betts would have to move to second, which is, in fact, his more natural position.  This would give us some options when it comes time to figure out what to do with Holt, who has proved his worth as more than just a utility man.  The resulting outfield would then consist of Nava, Castillo and Cespedes.

Scenario 3: “The irrationally optimistic scenario”.  Trade Bradley Jr., Cespedes and Castillo for Giancarlo Stanton.  This will probably never happen, but as Red Sox fans, we have grown accustomed to allowing ourselves’ imaginations to run a little too wild.  Miami has a tough time rallying their fan base.  Just look at their sparsely populated stadium next time they are on TV.  Even with a top 10 player in Stanton on the team, they still cannot excite themselves.  It took LeBron James to join the Miami Heat for that town to finally get excited about a sport.  With a large Cuban population, maybe two Cubans in the outfield will give the fans of Miami a reason to watch their team.  Who knows? All I can definitively say is that a trade of that nature would immediately improve the Red Sox and ultimately solve our overcrowded outfield.  Having said that, while Stanton only has one year remaining on his contract, there’s just no way the Red Sox could give them a solid offer that would not deplete our farm system.  It may also be in vain if Stanton decides to leave in free agency.

I choose scenario one.

The Bottom Line

Shane Victorino and Daniel Nava were forces to be reckoned with in 2013, but now, I’m afraid their trade value has plummeted due to lingering injuries and decreased production.  With a burgeoning young core that appears to be ready to contribute, along with two seemingly-formidable Cuban outfielders, Nava and Victorino have no place in the Sox starting lineup.  While one can come off the bench, it is clear that the other one must go.  While it is unfortunate that we cannot receive more for them through trade, it is clear that they are doing more harm than good with them as Red Sox players.  As a side note, Allen Craig’s abilities are really yet to be proven in a Sox uniform, so we’ll return to that at a later date.

When it comes to the infield, I think most Red Sox fans can agree with me that the optimism we had for Middlebrooks in 2012 was without merit, since he has shown that he cannot hit for average despite solid defense and the potential to hit for power.  While he is certainly not a huge factor in the Red Sox weak season, he is simply just not good enough to remain a starter for the Sox.  With Brock Holt showing that he can contribute offensively as well as defensively, I would substitute Holt in for Middlebrooks any day, despite his inferior power at the plate.

Wondering whether or not Holt or Betts will contribute to the next deep playoff run by the Red Sox is fruitless, since all we need to think about now is the near future and whether they’ll .  By showcasing them while also allowing them to acclimate to Major League play is crucial at this point.  While we do not want a Jackie Bradley Jr. situation to occur, being where we rushed him into the league, it certainly appears that both young talents will play just fine at this level.  If both of them pan out, who knows, maybe we can pry Giancarlo Stanton away from Miami.  Either way, if the Red Sox are going to fully commit themselves to an aggressive rebuild, which they apparently are, they’ll have to rid themselves of older talents (Nava is already 31!) and younger stick-in-the-muds (Middlebrooks) in order for it to be a successful in a swift rebuild.  An interesting trading partner in the future could be the Chicago Cubs, who have a great farm system stocked with position players, yet weak on pitching prospects, which, of course, we have plenty of.
