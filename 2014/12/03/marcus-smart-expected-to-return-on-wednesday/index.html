Marcus Smart Expected to Return on Wednesday

By Jerry DiStefano in Celtics, Featured, Opinion, Top Story ⋅ December 3, 2014 at 7:18pm ⋅ Post a comment

One day after blowing a 23-point road lead in Atlanta and extending their current losing streak to five games, the Celtics have finally received some good news. Marcus Smart is expected to come back from his 10-game injury absence on Wednesday at home against the Detroit Pistons. The Celtics have compiled an unimpressive 2-8 record during Smart’s absence, but his return hopefully will help alleviate some of their woes.

C’s head coach Brad Stevens called out his team about not being tough enough when faced with adversity. In an article by ESPN Boston’s Chris Forsberg, Stevens was quoted as saying, “When it comes easy, we’ve been good. When it gets tough, we haven’t. … Simple formula.”

Unfortunately, Stevens has some facts to back up his point through his team’s first 15 games this season. The Celtics are now 2-6 in games that are within one possession in the final minute and 2-7 in “clutch” games (a five-point difference in the final five minutes). So, how will the return of Smart help improve upon these horrific stats?

If you look of Smart’s stats on paper his return does not appear like it would have a huge effect for the Celtics. In five appearances, Smart is averaging 6.8 points, 2.6 rebounds, 1.2 assists and 1.2 steals over 20.4 minutes per game. What he does bring, even though he’s only a 20-year old rookie, is something the C’s are lacking, and that is toughness. Stevens was also quoted yesterday as noting how important toughness is when it comes to winning and losing.

“I’ve said this before and I believe it to be true: The game honors the more physical team,” Stevens said. “It does night in and night out. We’ve just got to improve in that area. … It is what it is. I’m not crazy enough to think that if it doesn’t change, we’ll be sitting up here a lot like this.”

In my opinion, toughness is one of Smart’s best intangibles as a basketball player. It can’t accurately be measured by statistics, only by wins and losses. If you look at a team like the Memphis Grizzlies who are categorized as a “tough team,” they currently sit with a 15-2 record, which is tied for the NBA’s best.

Despite Smart’s youth, I believe he is one of if not the toughest player on the team. Smart is not afraid to make a play in the most crucial moment of the game, whether that’s taking a shot or creating for someone else, and he wants to defend the opposing team’s best player, if that player happens to be a guard or small forward. Smart also has the ability to take off some of the pressure that Rajon Rondo has been facing in the fourth quarter of games during his absence.

Rondo’s updated fourth-quarter stat line this season: 108 minutes, 8-of-33 shooting (team-worst 24.2 percent), 32 assists, 13 turnovers, five blocked attempts, with a plus-minus of minus-50. Smart is the second-best ball handler on the team, and just that facet will help take an abundance of pressure off Rondo’s shoulders. Smart’s ability to not be afraid of making a big play will help Rondo immensely as well.

Although the Celts have a very small sample size, they are 2-3 with Smart and 2-8 without him. Smart being on the floor is going to make a difference, and that starts with Wednesday’s game.
